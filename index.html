🚀 Start here — the first five steps New v1.7

New to all of this? You don't need to read the whole guide today. These five steps give you the biggest safety improvement for the least effort — most people can finish them over a weekend. Each one links to the full explanation below.

  1. Get a password manager and let it create a different password for every account. Free · about 30 min
  2. Turn on two-factor authentication for your email and bank first — it stops most account break-ins even if your password leaks. Free · 10 min per account
  3. Freeze your credit at all three bureaus. It's free, reversible, and blocks most identity theft. Free · about 30 min
  4. Check if your email has been in a data breach at Have I Been Pwned, and change any password that shows up. Free · 5 min
  5. Run a free scan of what data brokers know about you with Optery, then start opting out — Section 3 walks you through it. Free scan · 15 min to start

Section 1  ·  Online Suggestions

These recommendations draw from my own practices, which I have found highly effective and straightforward to implement.

🔑 Get a Password Manager Reviews Setup Guide

  • Passwords are your first line of defense in protecting your online presence.
  • The rule of thumb: if you can remember your passwords, you are probably doing it wrong.
  • I primarily use Proton Pass (free and paid tier). BitWarden, 1Password, and KeePassXC (offline) are also excellent options.
  • Install a browser extension — e.g. Proton Pass for Chrome or Bitwarden for Firefox.
  • Bonus: safely store lock combinations, documents, notes, and important cards in your password manager.

Password Strength Tips

  • Minimum 16 characters; completely random words or letters.
  • Random: @JLfhD3KYDDKc32
  • Passphrase: meteor$DETROIT1wallow — just as strong, more typeable.
  • Avoid passwords referencing pets, kids, birthdates, or addresses — that info is often publicly available.

🔐 Use Two-Factor Authentication (2FA) — and Passkeys What is 2FA? How to use 2FA

Enable 2FA (also called MFA) on everything. Listed from least to most secure:

  • SMS / Email Code — Most common, least secure, but still better than nothing. A VOIP number is safer than your cell (see Section 2).
  • Authenticator Apps — More secure and convenient. I use Proton Authenticator and Yubico Authenticator. Also recommend Duo Mobile. These generate time-based 6-digit codes (TOTP) that change every 30 seconds.
    Your password manager can also manage TOTP codes — but use a separate Authenticator to protect the password manager itself. Protect your password manager with a passkey or physical security key wherever supported.
  • Physical Security Keys — Very secure. Small USB-like devices; touch a button to authenticate. I use the Yubikey 5NFC (USB-A + NFC) and Yubikey 5Ci (USB-C + Lightning). Browse models at Yubico, or compare options in the NYT Wirecutter security-key guide.
  • Passkeys 🆕 ★ Top Tier ★ — The gold standard when supported. A passkey replaces both your password and 2FA code with a single cryptographic key stored on your device (or in your password manager). There is no code to intercept, no phishing vector, and no server-side password database to breach.
    • Your device (phone, laptop, or security key) proves your identity using public-key cryptography. You authenticate with Face ID, Touch ID, or a PIN — nothing is ever sent to the website.
    • Where to use passkeys: passkeys.directory lists every site that currently supports them. Major adopters include Google, Apple, Microsoft, GitHub, PayPal, Amazon, and 1Password.
    • How to set them up: go to a supported site's security settings → look for 'Passkey' or 'Passwordless sign-in' → follow the prompt. Your password manager (1Password, Bitwarden, Proton Pass) can store passkeys alongside regular credentials.
    • When a site offers a passkey, choose it over any other 2FA option. Keep a physical security key as backup for your most critical accounts.

🛡 Use a VPN VPN Reviews

  • Hides your IP address and physical location.
  • Encrypts your web traffic, preventing man-in-the-middle attacks and blocking your ISP from seeing your activity.
  • Acts as a second (or third) barrier between your home network and hackers or identity thieves.
  • Quality VPNs include a mobile app for your smartphone.

☁ Set Up Encrypted Cloud Backup Cloud Backup Review

  • Backblaze — great for a single computer.
  • iDrive — more economical for multiple computers.
  • Set your own encryption key so only you can decrypt your data, even if the service is compromised.
Addendum — consumer cloud services: The big consumer clouds differ on who holds the key. OneDrive backups are encrypted, but you can only control the encryption key on professional/business plans. iCloud backups let you control the keys only if you turn on Advanced Data Protection, which is off by default.

🌐 Choose Privacy-First Browsers & Search Engines

Avoid Google Chrome and Microsoft Edge. Recommended alternatives:

  • Firefox and Brave — preferred browsers, both founded by privacy advocates.
  • DuckDuckGo and Brave Search — preferred search engines. Brave Search is the default, native search engine built into the Brave browser, so if you already use Brave you're using it by default.
  • SwissCows — family-friendly engine that automatically filters adult content.
  • Enable Global Privacy Control (GPC): GPC Guide

🖥 Assess Your Anti-Virus Posture

  • Running the latest Windows Defender? The free version of Malwarebytes is sufficient for most people — no separate paid anti-virus needed.
  • Paid suites like Norton, Bitdefender, and McAfee add value through bundled VPNs, child safety tools, and multi-device support. This added value can come at a cost: sometimes significant bloat that reduces speed and battery life.

❄ Freeze Your Credit Credit Freeze Basics Overview

A credit freeze is one of the most powerful defenses against identity theft. Unless you're actively applying for credit, keep it frozen.

  • Equifax · TransUnion · Experian
  • Free to freeze and unfreeze, as often as you like.
  • You can and should also freeze your minor children's credit.
  • Check your report: AnnualCreditReport.com is the free, official site that gives you one copy of your credit report from each of the three big bureaus (Equifax, Experian, TransUnion). Use it to spot accounts you didn't open. New v1.7

💳 Use Secure & Masked Payments

  • Apple Pay / Google Pay — Masks your real card number with a one-time code, protecting your info in vendor databases.
  • Privacy.com and Cloaked — Virtual card services. Cards are locked to specific vendors, can have spending limits, and can be deactivated any time.

👨‍👩‍👧‍👦 Family-Specific Protocols

Security habits work best when the whole household shares them. These protocols address the unique challenges of protecting children, teens, and elderly family members online.

Children & Teens

  • Freeze every child's credit at all three bureaus the moment they receive a Social Security Number. Child identity theft often goes undetected for years.
  • Use a family password manager (1Password Families, Bitwarden Organizations, or Proton Family) so parents can monitor and help children without sharing master passwords.
  • Create child accounts under parental supervision on all devices. On iOS use Screen Time; on Android use Family Link. Set app download approvals and daily limits.
  • Install a family-safe DNS filter on your home router: NextDNS (free tier available) or CleanBrowsing block adult content and malware domains before they load.
  • Teach children never to share their full name, school, address, or location in games, apps, or social media. Make this a regular conversation, not a one-time lecture.
  • Enable 2FA on gaming accounts (Roblox, Fortnite, Minecraft, Steam) — these are frequently targeted for credential theft.
  • For teens on social media, review privacy settings together annually. Use the NYT Social Media Privacy Guide as a checklist.

Elderly Family Members

  • Set up a password manager for them and offer to manage it. Proton Pass or Bitwarden have simple, clear interfaces. Store their recovery codes somewhere you both can access safely.
  • Enable 2FA on their email and bank accounts — these are the two highest-value targets for elder fraud.
  • Brief them on the most common elder scams: IRS/SSA imposters, Medicare fraud calls, grandparent scams, and romance scams. A simple rule: any caller demanding gift cards or wire transfers is a scammer, always.
  • Register their numbers on the National Do Not Call Registry and check it yearly. Consider a call-screening app like Nomorobo or their carrier's built-in spam filter.
  • Set up USPS Informed Delivery for them (see Section 2) so you or they can monitor physical mail for fraud.

Shared Household Practices

  • Hold an annual family security review — see the Annual Audit Checklist in Section 4.
  • Create a shared family emergency document (stored encrypted in your password manager) with: key account logins, who to contact if someone is locked out, and steps to take if a device is lost or stolen.
  • Use a guest Wi-Fi network for smart home devices (TVs, thermostats, cameras). Keep personal computers and phones on the main network. Most modern routers support this with a simple toggle.
📌 Family Security Quick Rules
  1. Never click links in unexpected emails or texts — go directly to the website instead.
  2. Anyone demanding urgency or secrecy is trying to scam you.
  3. When in doubt, hang up and call back on a number you look up yourself.
  4. Gift cards and wire transfers are never a legitimate form of payment to any government agency.

Section 2  ·  Privacy & Safety Resources

Each resource below has been selected based on its reputation as a safe and widely used tool. Many have alternatives — check CyberInsider for comparisons.

📧 Secure Personal Email

  • Proton Mail — Switzerland-based, strong encryption, free and paid plans. My primary secure email (I also bundle their VPN).
  • Tuta (Tutanota) — Another strong encrypted email option.

Disposable & Temporary Addresses

  • SimpleLogin and BurnerMail — Aliases to sign up for services without exposing your real email. Little to no cost.
  • Fastmail — Full-featured private email alternative.
  • One-time use: Guerrilla Mail and 10MinuteMail

📱 VOIP Numbers

  • Google Voice — Free secondary phone number, works from phone or computer.
  • MySudo — ~$1/month for an anonymous number and email.
  • Cloaked — Paid; includes masked email and phone.

VOIP numbers are more resistant to SIM-swapping attacks and let you keep your real number private.

💬 Secure Messaging Updated v1.7

  • Signal — Free, end-to-end encrypted, third-party audited. My top recommendation.
  • Also recommended: Wire Threema
  • WhatsApp — Usable for non-sensitive messaging, but feeds data directly to Meta. It remains a major tool for international messaging.
  • Telegram — ⚠ Popular, but regular chats are not end-to-end encrypted by default (only "Secret Chats" are, and only one-on-one). Don't treat it as a private messenger.
Removed in v1.7: BitChat, an experimental Bluetooth messaging app. It has no independent security audit, researchers found impersonation flaws, and fake copies of the app are circulating. Stick with Signal.

🔒 VPN Options Full VPN Reviews

  • Proton VPN — The only free VPN I recommend.
  • NordVPN — Well-regarded paid option.

🧩 Privacy Browser Extensions Updated v1.7

You only need one good content blocker — stacking several slows your browser and can break sites.

  • uBlock Origin — The gold standard: free, open source, blocks ads, trackers, and known malicious sites. Full version works on Firefox, Brave, and Edge. Install it only from your browser's official extension store, and check the developer page before installing — copycats exist.
  • On Google Chrome: Chrome no longer supports the full extension; use uBlock Origin Lite (a reduced version) — or better, switch to Firefox or Brave (see the browsers topic in Section 1). Brave also has strong blocking built in, no extension needed.
  • Privacy Badger — Optional companion from the EFF that learns to block sneaky trackers. Fine to run alongside uBlock Origin.
Removed in v1.7: HTTPS Everywhere was retired by the EFF — every major browser now does this automatically (in Firefox, turn on HTTPS-Only Mode under Settings → Privacy & Security). Also removed Adblock Plus (its "Acceptable Ads" program lets paying advertisers through) and Ghostery — uBlock Origin covers both of these functions, and does it better.

📬 Physical Mail Options

  • USPS Informed Delivery — Preview your mail digitally before it arrives. Free. FedEx and UPS have similar programs.
  • iPostal1, USGlobalMail, TravelingMailbox — Real physical addresses (not P.O. boxes) that can forward, shred, or scan your mail on demand.

🕵️ Dark Web & Breach Monitoring

Have I Been Pwned is a great starting point, but a layered approach gives you much earlier and broader coverage. The sources below monitor different parts of the breach ecosystem.

Free Monitoring Tools

  • Have I Been Pwned (HIBP) — The original. Checks your email against thousands of known public breaches. Enable free email alerts to be notified of new ones automatically.
  • Mozilla Monitor — Built on HIBP data but integrates directly into Firefox. Also surfaces data broker exposure and guides you through fixes. Free.
  • Proton Sentinel — For Proton Mail subscribers. Monitors your Proton account for suspicious access patterns and can lock it down automatically.
  • Experian — Includes a built-in free dark web monitoring service through its Protection Center. Scans the dark web for your personal info and alerts you to exposures.

Proactive Steps When You Find a Breach

  • Change the breached account's password immediately using your password manager to generate a new unique one.
  • If the breach included a password you reused elsewhere, change it on every affected site. This is why unique passwords per site matter so much.
  • If financial data was exposed, place a fraud alert with one credit bureau (it notifies all three) and consider a full credit freeze if you haven't already.
  • If your SSN was exposed, file an identity theft report at IdentityTheft.gov and consider enrolling in IRS Identity Protection PIN to prevent fraudulent tax returns.
  • Monitor your accounts daily for a few weeks after a breach using Credit Karma (free) or your bank's alerts.
Layer your monitoring: use at least one free service (HIBP + Mozilla Monitor) for basic coverage, and consider a paid service if you have dependents, hold high-value accounts, or have already been a victim of identity theft.

  • Norton SafeWeb — Check any website for malware, phishing, or virus activity.

Section 3  ·  Opting Out

The purpose of this section is to help you remove your personal identifying information from online data brokers and people-search sites.

🧹 Part 1 · Opt-Out Services and DIY Guides New v1.6

Paid services like the ones listed here will help get rid of somewhere between 40% and 80% of most people's digital privacy vulnerabilities. Doing the process yourself (DIY) can be much more time-consuming and technical, but will generally be far more effective in removing those digital privacy concerns. For people who are serious about their privacy, I usually advise a hybrid method, outlined below.

One strategy if you are new to digital privacy is to:

  1. Get a free report from Optery.
  2. Sign up for a paid service of your choice.
  3. Go through Part 2 · Data Brokers of this section to shut off the flow of information.
  4. After a month of the paid service, look at both your paid service's dashboard and your updated Optery dashboard, and use Parts 2 and 3 of this section to DIY-target the higher-priority individual opt-outs that have not been cleared by your paid service.
  5. Repeat step 4 every three to four months until you have achieved your digital privacy goals.

Start free: Before investing in any paid service, set up a free account with Optery to run an initial scan and see where your data is already exposed.

📋 Part 2 · Data Brokers

Primary Data Brokers (PDBs) gather online data from apps, websites, lenders, utilities, commercial entities, and social networks, then sell it to people-search sites and advertisers. Opting out of these PDBs is a critical step toward long-term improvement of your digital privacy. This step essentially shuts off a lot of the flows of information that people-search sites use to populate their files on you.

Section 4  ·  Annual Security Audit Checklist Updated v1.7

Run through this checklist once or twice a year — a weekend afternoon is enough. Check off items as you complete them.

Focus on the most critical items: password and 2FA audit, breach monitoring (HIBP, Mozilla), credit freezes and reports, data broker opt-outs, device security (updates, encryption, lock), and family emergency document. Run 1–2 times per year — but note that data-broker opt-outs benefit from a quarterly pass, since brokers re-list you (see Section 3).

📖 Plain-English Glossary New v1.7

Every technical term used in this guide, explained without jargon.

Password manager
An app that creates and remembers strong, unique passwords for you, locked behind one master password. You only memorize the one.
Two-factor authentication (2FA / MFA)
A second check when you log in — usually a code from your phone — so a stolen password alone isn't enough to get into your account.
Authenticator app / TOTP
A phone app that shows a 6-digit code that changes every 30 seconds. You type this code as your second login step. Safer than codes sent by text message.
Passkey
A newer way to sign in with no password at all — your phone or computer proves it's you using your fingerprint, face, or PIN. Nothing can be stolen from a website's password database because there's no password.
VPN (Virtual Private Network)
A service that hides your internet address and scrambles your traffic so your internet provider and public Wi-Fi snoopers can't watch what you do online.
Encryption
Scrambling data so only someone with the right key can read it. "End-to-end encrypted" means only you and the person you're talking to can read the messages — not even the company that runs the app.
Data broker
A company that quietly collects your personal details — name, address, phone, shopping habits — from many sources and sells them. You've probably never heard of them; they've heard of you.
People-search site
A website where anyone can type your name and see your address, phone number, relatives, and more, pulled from data brokers and public records. Section 3 shows how to remove yourself.
Opt-out
A formal request telling a data broker or people-search site to remove your information. Free, but they often re-add you — which is why repeating the process a few times a year matters.
Data breach
When hackers break into a company and steal its customer data — often including your email and password. Sites like Have I Been Pwned tell you if your accounts were caught in one.
Phishing
A fake email, text, or call pretending to be a company or person you trust, designed to trick you into giving up passwords or money. Urgency and secrecy are the biggest red flags.
SIM-swapping
A scam where a criminal convinces your phone carrier to move your number to their SIM card — then receives your text-message login codes. This is why app-based codes beat SMS codes.
VOIP number
A phone number that works over the internet (like Google Voice) instead of a SIM card. Handy as a "public" number so your real one stays private — and it can't be SIM-swapped.
Email alias
A disposable forwarding address that delivers to your real inbox. If a site leaks or spams it, you delete the alias — your real address was never exposed.
Credit freeze
A free lock on your credit file that stops anyone (including you) from opening new credit in your name until you temporarily lift it. The single strongest defense against identity theft.
